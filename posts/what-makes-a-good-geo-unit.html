What makes a good geo unit

November 2025 · 3 min read
geo experiments design data quality

One of the most under-discussed choices in geo experimentation is what geographic unit to actually use. Countries, regions, cities, postal codes — in principle any of these can work. In practice, the choice has a large effect on statistical power, spillover risk, and how much you trust the result.

The ideal geo unit is large enough to contain most of the effect you care about, small enough that you have enough of them to run a credible experiment, and internally coherent in the sense that user behaviour within a unit is more similar than behaviour across units. These three desiderata pull in different directions, and finding the right balance is mostly empirical.

Size and power

Larger units — countries or major regions — reduce spillover. A campaign running in France is unlikely to change booking behaviour in Germany. But if you only have ten units total, your experiment is under-powered and your randomisation is fragile. One unexpected shock to a single unit can swing the result.

Smaller units — cities or postal code clusters — give you more randomisation draws and better power. The tradeoff is that spillover becomes more plausible. People travel between nearby cities. Demand in one cluster is correlated with demand in adjacent ones. Whether this is a problem depends on the channel: paid search has relatively low local spillover, while brand campaigns or out-of-home media do not.

Variance and predictability

The other dimension is how predictable a unit's outcome is from its own history. High-variance units — places with seasonal spikes, one-off events, or thin data — will inflate your standard errors regardless of how many units you have. It is worth spending time understanding which regions are structurally noisy before you allocate them to treatment or control.

A useful exercise is to fit a simple time-series model to each unit using only pre-experiment data and look at the residuals. Units with large, unexplained residuals are risky to include. Either exclude them or pool them with a neighbouring stable unit if it makes geographic sense.

A rule of thumb

If you are working in a market where you have good coverage — say, the top 30–40 cities — and the treatment is channel-level (a specific campaign, a bid strategy change), city-level units usually strike a reasonable balance. If you are measuring something with wider reach, like a brand initiative or a pricing change, you may need to go broader. There is no universal answer, but the process of asking the question carefully will save you from the most common mistakes.
